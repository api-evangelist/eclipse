<span>Eclipse Conference #OCX26, one of those weeks that stays with you</span>

            <div class="field field--name-body field--type-text-with-summary field--label-hidden field--item"><p>Not just for the talks, but for what it revealed about where we are heading. I spent the week at <strong>Open Community Experience</strong> wearing multiple hats, as part of the community, and as a working group program manager across <strong>Open Community for Tooling </strong>scope, and it is one of those rare moments where you come back both energised and slightly unsettled, in a good way.&nbsp;</p><h3><strong>Regulation is now part of the picture.</strong></h3><p>The discussions around the <strong>CRA</strong> (Cyber Resilience Act) were not abstract. They were grounded, practical, and sometimes uncomfortable. #Opensource is not the issue. But responsibility becomes very real as soon as you are part of a commercial value chain.</p><p>And having regulators, foundations, and industry actors in the same room, thanks to Eclipse <a href="https://orcwg.org/"><strong>Open Regulatory Compliance</strong></a> Working Group changes the nature of the conversation.</p><p>The panel discussions around the Cyber Resilience Act, with contributions from <strong>Juan Rico</strong>, <strong>Mike Milinkovich</strong>, <strong>Johan Klykens</strong> , <strong>Lola Fernandez</strong> , <strong>Maika FOHRENBACH</strong>, made that very tangible.</p><div class="align-center">

<p>There is a clear shift happening, from interpretation to execution: btw, if you are looking for a concrete entry point, this is probably one of the most useful resources available: https://orcwg.org/cra/resources/. Thanks to<strong> Olle E Johansson</strong>, <strong>Shanda Giacomoni</strong> and the ORC team for these great insights. In short... "Don't panic" 😀, if you distribute software in the EU, addressing the security of your open source dependencies is no longer optional, and it goes well beyond simply generating an SBOM.</p><div class="align-center">

If you did not attend, you should definitely watch the replay!</p><div class="align-center">

<p><br />Another recurring discussion was around VS Code. Many teams are exploring alternatives, and some are even considering forking. But the deeper question is not really about the editor, it is about control, sustainability, and long term ownership of the stack you build on. I shared a more detailed perspective on this in a blog post a few months ago: <a href="https://blogs.eclipse.org/post/thomas-froment/why-cursor-windsurf-and-co-fork-vs-code-shouldnt">https://blogs.eclipse.org/post/thomas-froment/why-cursor-windsurf-and-co-fork-vs-code-shouldnt</a></p><div class="align-center">

<h3>Launch of the Open VSX Registry Service</h3><p>The announcement of the Open VSX Registry service by <strong>Gaël Blondelle</strong> and <strong>Thabang Mashologu</strong>, and its growing adoption across the ecosystem, including players like Cursor, Google, AWS and others, we are clearly moving into a world where the extension ecosystem is part of the product experience.</p><div class="align-center">
  
  
  <div class="field field--name-field-media-image field--type-image field--label-visually_hidden">
    <div class="field--label sr-only">Image</div>
              <div class="field--item">  <img alt="Gael Blondelle and Thabang Mashologu presenting on stage at Open Community for Tooling, with a slide explaining that the registry is now part of the product experience, highlighting availability, performance, and reliability as key priorities." class="img-responsive" height="358" src="https://blogs.eclipse.org/sites/default/files/styles/large/public/2026-04/OpenVSX-GaelAndThabang.png?itok=FMg2KXkv" width="480" />


</div>
          </div>

</div>
<p>Not something you just consume. Something you need to understand, you may have a look at https://managed.open-vsx.org/ to know more about it!</p><p>It is important to note that Open VSX acts as the neutral distribution layer for VS Code-compatible extensions, enabling a vendor-independent extensibility ecosystem. The Open VSX registry service is designed for any tool that supports this extensibility format &amp; API, not only VS Code derivatives. This includes platforms such as Eclipse Theia IDE (which is not a VS Code fork), as well as products like Google Antigravity and many others.</p><h3>Eclipse Platform, RCP and IDE modernisation</h3><p>It was one of the more nuanced themes throughout the week:</p><p>The <a href="https://github.com/swt-initiative31">initiative 31</a> report presented by <strong>Heiko Klare</strong> showed solid experimentation, and valuable learnings around the future of Eclipse IDE and RCP. At the same time, it is currently on hold due to a lack of contributors, which says a lot about the reality of sustaining such efforts.&nbsp;</p><div class="align-center">

The work of <strong>Sebastian Sampaoli</strong>'s team on migrating and modernising RCP applications clearly resonates with real needs. Different approaches, different starting points, but pointing to the same underlying reality: there is demand, there is urgency, and no single solution yet.</p><div class="align-center">

<h3>OCX brings together innovation and production-ready technologies</h3><p>From production-proven technologies like Eclipse 4diac (credits to <strong>Alois Zoitl </strong>and <strong>Michael Oberlehner</strong>), powering hundreds of systems and millions of devices (sometimes billion of devices for Eclipse ThreadX!), to emerging projects in incubation like the Eclipse Open Collaboration Tool, presented by <strong>Jan Bicker </strong>and<strong> Miro Spönemann</strong>, the conference highlights the full spectrum of open source innovation. A great illustration of how mature systems and new ideas evolve together within the same community.</p><div class="align-center">
